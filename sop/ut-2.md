I don't claim to be making technical decisions for the directorate at this point in my career, but no rank or experience level can prevent me from contributing to the discussion. That being said, no matter how you slice it, it is difficult to "prompt" or "Google" for the tradeoff analysis without some minimum level of expertise in the theory of the alternatives. Knowing what aspects of a technical solution to consider further and how is a problem of depth and can be addressed by studying, formally, the nature of computer science and all of its branches. My intention is to explore this deeply enough not just to understand it, but to communicate it to all stakeholders correctly and succinctly.

My undergraduate degree in computer science provided me wonderful breadth in topics of computer science, including special interest electives like parallel computing, cybersecurity law, and advanced cryptography. Today, I can conclude that the MSCSO curriculum contains subject matter of particular relevance and interest to my personal and professional life that will afford me the depth I so desire. 

A constant has been my fascination with algorithms and the theory of computation. During my undergraduate degree, I joined a research lab exploring models of dynamic programming structures in connected agents that greatly shaped my view of computer science outside of pure coding. Hence, I am pleased to see the Algorithms course in the curriculum.

One unexpected fascination that only took roots after joining industry was with the implementations of programming languages. Having attended school in which Bjarne Stroustrup (inventor of C++) was a professor, I obviously became familiar with object-oriented programming and other unique features of the C++ language. However, the language used at my job - Ada - could not be more different. Similar to spoken language, this disconnect forced me to immerse myself in the aspects of programming languages that I would have taken for granted, so to say, to become an effective engineer. Automata, parsing, and linking are just three topics introduced in undergrad that have become pertinent to my personal understanding of the technologies in my industry. It's no surprise that I view the Implementation of Programming Languages course with great excitement. 

The coursework in the MSCSO will have an immediate impact on my work performance as I intend to complete it while working full-time. I do not take this for granted; the opportunity to immediately use knowledge gained from a reputable institution to innovate is elating. I am no stranger to high workloads, having taken large courseloads in university and graduating Magna Cum Laude while working my on-campus part-time job. 

Any problem worth solving, whether it is in software engineering or another aspect of computer science, will require me to open my toolbox and fit the puzzle pieces together.
