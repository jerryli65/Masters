There is a common joke that software in aerospace and defense is 2 years of development and 15 years of maintenance. While the numbers are fudged somewhat, there is certainly truth to this; I experienced such during my co-op rotations in undergrad, where I alternated semesters between Texas A&M University in College Station, TX and Collins Aerospace in Cedar Rapids, IA. Flash forward about 3 years since my first co-op, as a full-time software engineer, and my department at Collins is entering the 2-years side of the coin (give or take some years). Very quickly, I have realized that there is a false equivalency between simple and basic. While aerospace software needs to be as simple as possible to comply with certification objectives from the certification authorities such as the Federal Aviation Administration, the gap that must be bridged between the design and the implementation is anything but basic. I have observed senior engineers display eloquence, innovation, and substantial theoretical knowledge to make critical decisions regarding how to ensure the plane flies safely. I believe that these are the technical traits that I lack at this stage of my career and will provide the backbone that allows me to be highly effective in building my product.

Eloquence. I believe that concluding the path forward in a technical design review comes not from the knowledge of why the solution is perfect, but why the solution is better than the alternatives. It is difficult to "prompt" or "Google" for the tradeoff analysis without fundamental expertise in the theory of the alternatives. Knowing *what* to Google is a problem of depth and can be addressed by studying, formally, the nature of computer science and all of its branches. My intention is to explore this deeply enough not just to understand it, but to communicate it to all stakeholders correctly and succinctly.

Confidence and innovation. Effective communication relies on confidence of personal eptitude. Increasing the size of my toolbox to be able to hold a technical conversation face-to-face without having to stop and ask about every technical aspect is a confidence booster. Further, I will feel ready to contribute to such discussions and even propose new innovation. Diving deeper into a technical field provides historical relevance to the topic at hand and provides a springboard for innovation as the landscape continues to evolve.

Theoretical knowledge. In undergrad, I was abler to explore *breadth* of topics such as artificial intelligence, parallel computing, cryptography, and cybersecurity law. What I now desire is to explore the _depth_ of topics that are continuously relevant in my industry. The systems courses in particular have direct application to the embedded nature of aviation electronics. Having a deeper cognizance of computer systems will allow me to understand the theory of the hardware resources at our disposal. In undergrad, I dove into algorithmic theory in the research lab I participated in, where I modelled dynamic programming in connected agents. This is probably the computer science topic I find the most fascinating, so I am pleased to see that the curriculum includes higher-level algorithms and computation theory.

The coursework in the MSCSO will have an immediate impact on my work performance as I intend to complete it while working full-time. I do not take this for granted; the opportunity to immediately use knowledge gained from a reputable institution to innovate is elating. I am no stranger to high workloads, having taken high courseloads in university while working my on-campus part-time job. My personal success in this program and the reputational brilliance of the University of Texas are of highest priority for the next few years. This is the backbone to which I will be able to dive closer to the code and deeper in the theory to eventually realize my professional goal of becoming an individual contributor within my corporation. 

Ultimately, there are things that I would have done differently in undergrad if I were to begin again today.
